An Anciente Mappe of Fairyland

A guided journey through Bernard Sleigh's 1917 map.

By Elizabeth Schaefer, CSTC 2310

An Anciente Mappe of Fairyland was published in 1917.

It was created by English artist Bernard Sleigh.

At nearly six feet long, it brings together myths, fairy tales, and children's heroes all in one map.

Let's zoom in.

English chivalric legend appears with figures such as

King Arthur, Lancelot, and Excalibur.

German fairy tales are woven in as well.

Figures like Rapunzel, Hansel and Gretel, and the Seven Dwarves bring the stories of the Brothers Grimm into the map.

Greek myths emerge,

with the Hydra, Cerberus, the Labyrinth, the Golden Fleece, and a smattering of Greek gods.

Even anti-Welsh rhymes come into play.

All of these characters come from Eurocentric tales,
uniting different European traditions into one shared imaginative world.
Sleigh emphasizes this lack of cohesive heritage with some of these fantastical details:

For example,

Narcissus and Satyrs reside alongside

Cinderella, Jack and Jill, and Little Red Riding Hood.

The English classic Peter Pan's house,

looks out onto Danish author Hans Christian Anderson's The Little Mermaid.

Even the compass rose blends cartographic order with fantasy:

There are directions such as North of Nowhere

and East of the Sun.

The distance on the map is measured by "Thoughts,"

with joking nods towards pictorial scale.

The compass rose looks like the tool of a real map, promising order and orientation, but reveals further magic.
We see similar treatment with the map legend.

These landmarks (wishing wells, elfin temples, fairie shrines) are presented as ordinary features of the landscape,

furthering the sense of logical enchantment within the map.

The map of Fairyland suggests that all human stories and fairy tales are connected within one world.

In the introduction of the guide to Fairyland, there is this verse:

In Fairyland, this world of imagination becomes truly spatial.

The inner mind of a dreaming child can be entered, traversed, and even mapped.

If it can be mapped, then maybe it even exists... somewhere.

An Anciente Mappe of Fairyland was published in 1917, while World War I was still underway.

Sleigh later wrote that "the Peter Pan in me emerged in full strength" during this time, "chiefly I suppose as a mental refuge from the hideous militarism of the time."

Fairyland, then, is more than escapism.

It is the child's imaginative world remade as a shelter,

where the biggest worries are not bombs or tear gas, but dragons and witches in woods.

A landscape of fantasy becomes a refuge from the violence of the adult world,

a return to a Never Never Land.

"And so, farewell; until that day of days when you and I may meet together there." - Bernard Sleigh, An Anciente Mappe of Fairyland.
